# Q3: Motion_and_Artifact_Handling

**(a) Handling of Head Motion and Physiological Noise in Naturalistic fMRI**

Despite the complexity of naturalistic paradigms, one of their primary methodological advantages is their ability to implicitly minimize head motion. Especially in challenging populations, such as children or cognitively impaired patients, movie-watching serves as a behavioral constraint that reduces anxiety and significantly lowers in-scanner head movement compared to unconstrained resting-state fMRI [1-4]. In fact, compliance rates and head motion parameters—such as framewise displacement (FD)—improve markedly during movies, particularly for children under the age of 10 [4, 5]. 

However, excessive head motion still remains the leading cause of scan failure across populations [6, 7]. To handle motion and physiological noise during naturalistic viewing, researchers deploy several rigorous correction pipelines:
*   **Motion Parameter Regression and Thresholding:** Studies standardly employ rigid-body transformations (e.g., MCFLIRT) to align images, followed by the regression of 6, 12, or 24 head motion parameters (such as the Friston-24 model, which includes the 6 movement parameters of the current volume, the 6 from the preceding volume, and their squares) [8-11]. Researchers typically calculate framewise displacement (FD) and exclude subjects or "scrub" volumes that exceed thresholds, such as an FD > 0.15 mm, 0.3 mm, or 0.5 mm [9, 12-14].
*   **Physiological Noise Correction:** Because respiration and heart rate induce robust low-frequency fluctuations in the BOLD signal (particularly around the cerebral midline and default mode network), studies often record these signals using pneumatic chest belts and pulse oximeters [15-17]. These signals are then modeled and regressed out as nuisance variables [18]. Additionally, researchers regress out signals from the white matter (WM) and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) using techniques like CompCor to eliminate non-neuronal physiological variance [8, 13, 18].

**(b) The Debate: Pure Confound vs. Informative Signal for Engagement and Arousal**

Historically, the dominant perspective has treated physiological fluctuations and motion as pure confounds that generate spurious functional connectivity. Methodologies have been highly focused on aggressively scrubbing and filtering out signals associated with cardiac rhythms, respiration, and movement to ensure that connectivity reflects true intrinsic or stimulus-evoked neuronal activity [8, 11, 13, 18, 19].

However, the current debate recognizes that **physiological signals and motion during naturalistic viewing are not merely noise, but carry rich, informative data regarding subjective engagement, affective states, and cognitive arousal** [20-23]. Naturalistic stimuli evoke autonomic nervous system responses—such as changes in heart rate (HR) and pupil diameter (PD)—that tightly couple with dynamic transitions in brain states [20, 21, 24]. 
*   **Arousal and Network Cohesion:** Continuous physiological monitoring during arousing movie clips demonstrates that HR (a physiological index of arousal) exhibits a monotonically increasing relationship with the cohesion of the brain's **salience network** [25, 26]. Strikingly, interactions between the salience network and the executive control network peak at *moderate* levels of arousal (indicated by HR), revealing that autonomic physiological signals dynamically track how the brain reprioritizes large-scale network communication under stress [25, 27, 28].
*   **Pupillometry and Engagement:** Pupil diameter covaries with specific brain state transitions. For instance, larger PD has been associated with brain states involving high executive and sensory network activity, while smaller PD relates to visual states processing faces, mapping physiological changes to real-time narrative engagement and surprise [20, 24]. 
Consequently, while idiosyncratic physiological variations are aggressively controlled for, the stimulus-locked autonomic efferents are increasingly embraced as valuable multimodal markers of fear, suspense, and cognitive immersion [22, 23].

**(c) Deep-Learning and Adaptive Solutions for Artifacts and Modeling**

To address the complex artifact and physiological challenges inherent to naturalistic fMRI, the field has developed several advanced adaptive, deep-learning, and data-driven solutions:

*   **Motion-Corrected Eye Tracking (MoCET):** Integrating eye-tracking into fMRI is notoriously difficult because standard models cannot distinguish gaze shifts from head movements, ruining calibration. **MoCET** is a novel adaptive solution that applies 6-Degree-of-Freedom (6-DoF) head motion parameters derived from the fMRI data itself, combined with polynomial regressors, to retroactively correct gaze drift. MoCET isolates true eye movements without requiring additional in-scanner hardware, outperforming conventional detrending methods and enabling high-precision eye tracking across multiple naturalistic scanning runs [29-32].
*   **DeepMReye:** As an alternative to camera-based tracking, **DeepMReye** is a deep convolutional neural network model that decodes gaze coordinates directly from the MR signals of the eyeball. While it successfully captures the general gaze trajectory and eliminates the need for physical eye-tracking cameras, evaluations suggest it may currently lack the fine spatial precision provided by camera-based systems like MoCET [33-35].
*   **Self-Supervised / Data-Driven Artifact Removal:** Tools such as **ICA-AROMA** (Independent Component Analysis-based Automatic Removal of Motion Artifacts) and **ICA+FIX** use data-driven, independent component analysis to automatically identify and aggressively remove motion artifacts and non-neuronal spatiotemporal components from the fMRI signal without needing external physiological recordings [11, 36, 37].
*   **Inter-Subject Functional Correlation (ISFC):** ISFC operates as a powerful mathematical filter for naturalistic paradigms. By calculating the correlations *between* the brains of different subjects watching the exact same movie, ISFC automatically isolates stimulus-evoked neural activity while successfully filtering out both intrinsic neural dynamics and idiosyncratic non-neuronal artifacts (e.g., individual head motion, respiration, and HR), since these confounds are not correlated across different individuals [38-40].
*   **Dual-Adaptive Filtering for Speech:** For experiments involving naturalistic spoken recall inside the scanner, dual-adaptive filters are utilized. A reference microphone captures background scanner noise, which the algorithm uses to continuously learn filter gains and adaptively subtract noise from the source microphone (the participant's speech), achieving real-time speech enhancement [41-43]. 
*   **Temporal Derivative Distribution Repair (TDDR):** For related neuroimaging modalities like fNIRS, TDDR is an adaptive motion correction technique that projects data onto a Principal Component Analysis (PCA) space to repair signal spikes and baseline shifts induced by subject motion during naturalistic interactions [44].
